Knitting Around the World

Knitting as a practice of creating textiles is found globally. It's roots are in the Arabic world where it came to Europe via Spain during the Crusades. The oldest surviving knit is a pair of Coptic socks from 11th century Egypt. Prior to this, nålbinding, a similar practice that uses one needle instead of two was used globably. The oldest textiles created useing nålbinding have been found in the Judean Desert (6580 BCE) and Denmark (4280 BCE). Knitting and nålbinding are similar because they are both knott-less ways of creating fabric using loops. After the spread of knitting, different cultures took the craft and created different folkways and traditions centered around the garments they created. Here we'll take a look at a few different examples from around the world

Scottish Knitting

Knitting is a crucial part of Scottish folk history and the nation's identity. As a country that produces some of the finest wool, it would only be right that there was a development of several national styles. Knitting was crucial to the Scottish economy from the 18th to the 20th century. The most prominent knit styles in Scottland come from the Shettland Islands. It is from these islands that we get Shetland lace and the Fair Isle knit style. Shetland lace is an open-work style knit that mimicks true lace. Fair Isle patterns are a form of color-work that use exactly two different colors of yarn per row to create a beautiful fabric.

Irish Knitting

Much like Scottland, Ireland is a huge producer of wool. The most predominant knit style associated with Ireland is the Aran jumper, which uses cable knitting to create intreicate three dimensional patterns within the fabric.

English Knitting

Knitting has been a long time part of English history, with a style of knitting named after the country. An import historical knit in English history is the Gurnsey, a hardy knit sweater worn by fishermen. The top of the sweater is decorated with different stitch patterns while the bottom three-quarters where kept plain in order to make mending easy.

Swedish Knitting

There are several knitting traditions in Sweden. Historically color work has been an important folk-tradition, with different patterns representing different counties of the country. In the 20th century the Bohuslan style was started by women looking to financially support themselves. These sweaters feature colorful patterns done around the yoke of the sweater.

Norwegian Knitting

The most famous Norwegian knitting style is the lusekofte, or lice sweater. The style was named after lice for the small white specks that adorn the body. This style of knitting allowed for a garment that was double knit to be extra warm. These sweaters feature ornate color work in patterns that have historical meanings. The are knit in black or navy blue with white color work and sometimes feature a red band around the collar.

Icelandic Knitting

The lopapyesa is the traditional Icelandic knit style. It features color work done around the yoke. Iceland is known for its superb wool production.

Andean Knitting

AN important part of Andean knitting tradition is the Ch'ulu, or a knitted cap. These caps are made from alpaca fiber and knitting in ornate and colorful patterns.
